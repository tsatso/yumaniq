# 1. What RAST Does

RAST (Robotic Adaptive Skill Transfer) extracts the optimization strategy behind expert motor behavior and transfers it to new conditions where the original behavior would fail.

The core distinction: Traditional approaches capture what experts DO (their movements, trajectories, demonstrated behaviors). RAST captures what experts OPTIMIZE FOR (their objectives, trade-offs, and priorities). Movements are specific to conditions. Objectives are general across conditions.

Example: An expert excavator operator demonstrates a loading technique on dry gravel. Traditional methods (Learning from Demonstration, video capture) record that specific arm trajectory. On wet clay the next day, that trajectory fails because the material resists differently. RAST would have extracted that the expert was optimizing for "maximize fill volume while minimizing passes and bucket stress." That objective generates a different, adapted trajectory for wet clay automatically, without the expert needing to demonstrate in every condition.

# 2. Scientific Foundation

## 2.1 Optimal Feedback Control (Todorov and Jordan, 2002)

The established theory that biological movements are not memorized sequences but real-time solutions to optimization problems. The motor system computes the best action given the current state, the goal, and the constraints. Key principles:

- Minimum Intervention Principle: only correct deviations that affect the task goal. Deviations that don't matter are left alone.
- Signal-Dependent Noise: motor commands produce proportional noise, so the system optimizes for robustness, not just accuracy.
- Redundancy Exploitation: extra degrees of freedom serve as noise buffers, not wasted capacity.

Implication: if the brain optimizes, then behavior is generated by a cost function. If we can infer that cost function, we understand WHY the person moves the way they do, not just HOW they move.

## 2.2 Inverse Optimal Control (IOC)

The mathematical method for inferring cost functions from observed behavior. Given:
- Observed behavior (what the person did)
- A dynamics model (what's physically possible)
- A set of candidate cost function features (what objectives might be relevant)

IOC finds the weight vector w* over those features such that the observed behavior is near-optimal under the resulting cost function:

J = integral of sum(w_i * feature_i(state, control)) dt

IOC is established science, not a RAST invention. It has been applied to reaching movements (Mombaur, Jin, Kulic), locomotion, and manipulation. RAST's contribution is not IOC itself but what is done with the output.

Key constraint: IOC does not discover features. It only recovers weights over a predefined feature set. Feature selection is a modeling choice. If the feature set is wrong, the weights are meaningless.

IOC implementation in RAST uses bilevel optimization via CMA-ES:
1. Propose candidate weight vector (domain-agnostic, black-box optimizer)
2. Forward-solve: given these weights and a dynamics model, generate what optimal behavior would look like (domain-specific, requires physics model)
3. Compare the forward-solved optimal trajectory to the observed behavior
4. Update the weight proposal based on the discrepancy
5. Repeat until convergence

Three of four phases are domain-agnostic. Only the forward solve requires domain-specific physics. This is what makes the pipeline reusable across applications.

## 2.3 The Berniker Gap (2013)

Berniker et al. tested whether cost functions generalize across settings (e.g., does the same person optimize for the same thing when reaching in different force fields?).

Finding: qualitative trends matched (people who prioritized accuracy in one condition tended to prioritize accuracy in another). Quantitative predictions failed (the exact weight values didn't transfer).

Conclusion: "Could neither conclude... nor rule out" that cost functions are conserved.

This is the open scientific question that RAST addresses.

## 2.4 RAST's Novel Contribution: The Decomposition Hypothesis

Prior work asked: "Is the cost function conserved?" (yes/no, binary)

RAST asks: "Which components of the cost function are conserved (trait-like) and which adapt to context (state-like)?"

This reframes the question from monolithic transfer to component-wise decomposition.

- Trait components: stable preferences that persist across conditions. Example: an operator who consistently prioritizes smooth motion over speed will do so in any terrain. This is part of their motor identity.
- State components: context-dependent adjustments that change when conditions change. Example: the same operator increases their caution weight when operating on a slope versus flat ground. This is adaptation, not identity.

Berniker's quantitative predictions failed because the methodology treated cost functions as monolithic. If some weights are traits (stable) and others are states (variable), then monolithic transfer would show exactly the pattern Berniker observed: qualitative similarity but quantitative mismatch.

The decomposition hypothesis predicts that if you transfer only the trait components and allow state components to recalibrate in the new context, quantitative prediction improves.

This is testable. It has not yet been proven or disproven.

# 3. The RAST Pipeline

## Step 1: Capture

Observe the expert performing the task across multiple conditions. Conditions must vary in controlled ways to enable decomposition (e.g., different terrain types, different loads, different time pressures, different environmental conditions).

Required data: kinematics (joint angles, positions, velocities), and ideally kinetics (forces, torques). The richer the sensor data, the better the identifiability of the cost function.

For heavy equipment: this could come from CAN bus telemetry (joint angles, hydraulic pressures), GPS (bucket position), IMU (machine orientation), and load sensors. Modern equipment from manufacturers like Caterpillar, Komatsu, and Volvo CE already generates much of this data through systems like Cat Product Link, Komatsu KOMTRAX, and Volvo CareTrack.

## Step 2: Extract Kinematic Invariants

Before running IOC, extract stable patterns in the expert's behavior within each condition:
- Speed profile shape (single-peaked vs. multi-peaked, symmetric vs. asymmetric)
- Correction signatures (micro-adjustments near target, timing of corrections)
- Coordination couplings (which joints move together, compensation patterns)
- Variability patterns (where trial-to-trial variation concentrates)

These invariants serve as constraints on the IOC solution, resolving ambiguity when multiple weight vectors could explain the same trajectory.

For heavy equipment: invariants might include approach angle consistency, bucket curl timing relative to penetration depth, swing deceleration profiles, and load-dependent arm coordination patterns.

## Step 3: Infer Objectives via IOC

Run the IOC solver to find the weight vector that makes the observed behavior near-optimal.

Typical cost function features for manipulation tasks:
- Accuracy: distance from target position
- Effort: sum of squared joint torques or control inputs
- Smoothness: jerk (third derivative of position) minimization
- Time: total task duration
- Force control: deviation from desired contact force
- Path efficiency: actual path length divided by shortest possible path

For heavy equipment specifically, additional features might include:
- Fill efficiency: bucket fill volume relative to capacity
- Ground disturbance: unwanted terrain displacement
- Machine stress: forces on structural components relative to limits
- Fuel consumption: energy expenditure per unit of work
- Stability margin: distance from tipping boundary

The output is a weight vector with uncertainty: w_accuracy = 0.35 plus or minus 0.08, w_smoothness = 0.65 plus or minus 0.04, etc. Each weight quantifies how much the expert prioritizes that objective relative to others.

## Step 4: Couple IOC with Invariants

Only accept weight vectors that explain both the trajectories AND the invariant patterns. This resolves identifiability problems where multiple weight vectors could fit the trajectory data alone.

Example: IOC returns two candidate weight sets. The expert shows consistent late corrections and multi-peaked velocity profiles. Weight-set A predicts smooth single-shot movements (contradicts invariants). Weight-set B predicts corrective adjustments (matches invariants). Keep B, reject A.

## Step 5: Decompose into Trait vs. State

This is the core novel step. Given the same expert's weights across multiple conditions, classify each weight:

- Low variance across contexts (ICC > 0.75): TRAIT. This is a stable preference. It transfers.
- High variance across contexts (ICC < 0.40): STATE. This adapts to conditions. It needs recalibration in new contexts.
- Intermediate variance: MIXED. Partially stable. Transfer the stable component, recalibrate the rest.

Classification uses Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC), coefficient of variation, temporal stability analysis, and context-condition correlation.

Minimum data requirement: the same expert performing in at least 3 distinct conditions (e.g., different materials, different terrain, different loads) with multiple repetitions per condition.

Example decomposition for a hypothetical excavator operator:

| Weight | Across conditions | Classification |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| w_smoothness (movement quality) | 0.65 plus or minus 0.03 | TRAIT |
| w_fill_efficiency (bucket fullness) | 0.70 plus or minus 0.05 | TRAIT |
| w_speed (task duration) | 0.40 plus or minus 0.20 | STATE |
| w_ground_disturbance (terrain preservation) | 0.55 plus or minus 0.15 | MIXED |

Interpretation: this operator consistently prioritizes smooth movements and full bucket scoops regardless of conditions (trait). They adjust their speed and terrain care based on the specific situation (state). Transferring their smoothness and fill efficiency priorities to a new context or to an autonomous system preserves what makes them effective.

## Step 6: Transfer via OFC

For a new context (different terrain, different machine, remote operation, autonomous execution):

1. Take the expert's trait weights as the objective function
2. Plug in the new dynamics model (the new machine, the new conditions)
3. Forward-solve using Optimal Feedback Control to generate the adapted behavior
4. The result is what the expert WOULD do in this new context if their preferences were preserved

This is the transfer step. The trait weights are physics-independent (the expert always values smooth motion and full scoops). The resulting trajectory is physics-dependent (how you achieve smooth motion and full scoops depends on the specific machine and conditions). OFC bridges between the two.

# 4. RAST Architecture (C2 Platform)

## Components

- MPU (Motor Processing Unit): extracts Motor Identity from sensor data. Runs IOC, extracts invariants, performs decomposition. This is the extraction engine.
- MIM (Motor Intelligence Model): vector-native storage where Motor Identities are embedded. Distance equals similarity, direction equals transformation. Population-level patterns emerge from embedding structure.
- Solver: generates adapted movement commands. Can be full OFC or simpler gradient-based methods depending on domain maturity.
- Simulator: tests and verifies generated behavior before deployment.
- Safety Governor: enforces hard bounds at 1kHz deterministic rate. No command ever violates safety limits regardless of what the Solver proposes.
- Adapter: translates Solver output to specific hardware APIs.

## Pipeline

Sense (sensors) -> Process (MPU) -> Store (MIM) -> Solve -> Simulate -> Govern -> Adapt -> Execute

## Domain-Agnostic vs. Domain-Specific

The core engine (IOC solver, decomposition, prediction validation, CMA-ES optimization) is domain-agnostic. It works identically for surgical skills, musical performance, cardiac rehabilitation, or heavy equipment operation.

The domain-specific components are: the dynamics model (physics of the specific machine/body), the cost features (what objectives are relevant in this domain), and the data loader (how sensor data gets converted to the standard trajectory format).

This means: validate the engine once, deploy to any domain by swapping the physics model and cost features.

# 5. What RAST Captures That Existing Methods Do Not

## Comparison with current knowledge transfer approaches

### Learning from Demonstration (LfD) / Imitation Learning
What it captures: the expert's trajectory (what they did)
Representation level: policy (state-to-action mapping)
Transfer capability: reproduces the demonstrated behavior. Fails when conditions change because the policy is specific to the conditions under which it was demonstrated.
What it misses: WHY the expert chose that trajectory. The underlying optimization strategy that would generate a different, adapted trajectory under different conditions.

### Virtual Reality (VR) Capture
What it captures: the expert's movements in a specific simulated scenario
Representation level: kinematics in a single context
Transfer capability: documents behavior in one scenario. Does not generalize because the captured movements are optimal only for that specific geometry, material, and condition set.
What it misses: the objective function that generated those movements. A different scenario requires re-demonstration.

### Eye-Tracking and EEG (Brain-Computer Interfaces)
What it captures: where the expert looks and neural activation patterns
Representation level: sensory attention, not motor decision-making
Transfer capability: can train novices where to look. Cannot train novices what to decide based on what they see.
What it misses: the causal link between perception and motor action. Knowing the expert looked at the bucket edge does not explain why they chose a specific descent rate.

### Expert Systems (If-Then Rules)
What it captures: what the expert can verbalize about their decision-making
Representation level: explicit, articulable knowledge only
Transfer capability: codifies known rules. Cannot capture what experts cannot articulate, which is the majority of expert performance.
What it misses: tacit knowledge. Research confirms that expert operators "often have difficulty verbally explaining their split-second decisions and physical strategies."

### Video Documentation
What it captures: visual record of expert performing tasks
Representation level: raw observation
Transfer capability: shows what happened. Does not structure, explain, or enable adaptation.
What it misses: which moments contain critical decisions versus routine operation. Produces hours of unfocused content.

### RAST / IOC with Decomposition
What it captures: the expert's optimization strategy (cost function weights) decomposed into stable traits and context-dependent states
Representation level: the objective function itself, the deepest layer of motor expertise
Transfer capability: trait weights transfer to new conditions. New dynamics model plus preserved objectives generates adapted behavior automatically. The expert does not need to demonstrate in every possible condition.
What it misses: RAST requires a dynamics model of the target system. If the physics of the new machine or environment cannot be modeled, transfer cannot be computed. RAST also assumes the expert's behavior is near-optimal, which is valid for experienced operators but not for novices or learners.

# 6. Honest Limitations

## What is established science
- OFC explains motor behavior well (Todorov and Jordan, 2002, widely replicated)
- IOC can recover cost functions from observed behavior (Mombaur, Jin, Kulic, multiple groups)
- Individuals show consistent motor styles within contexts (robust finding)
- Qualitative trends transfer across contexts (Berniker confirmed)

## What is the RAST hypothesis (unproven)
- Whether quantitative prediction across contexts is achievable via decomposition
- Whether trait/state decomposition produces a clean separation
- Whether trait weights genuinely predict behavior in novel contexts better than generic baselines

## What could fail
- Decomposition might not produce clean trait/state separation (cost functions might be more context-entangled than hypothesized)
- Transfer might require more recalibration than expected (reducing the advantage over simpler methods)
- The dynamics model for heavy equipment might be insufficiently accurate for IOC to converge reliably

## The core testable claim
"When we transfer trait weights to a new system, does behavior preserve expert objectives better than a generic policy or a directly transferred LfD policy?"

If yes: RAST provides something no current method can, the transferable essence of expertise.
If no: RAST provides a starting point for optimization but does not fundamentally outperform LfD.

This is testable. It has not yet been tested on heavy equipment data.

# 7. Application to Heavy Equipment Operations

## The problem RAST addresses in this domain

Expert heavy equipment operators develop optimization strategies over 15-20 years that include: how to leverage machine compliance for full bucket scoops, when to strategically ignore minor oscillations to maximize speed, how to adapt approach angle based on material properties, and how to coordinate complex multi-joint movements for underactuated tools.

Current knowledge transfer methods capture surface-level behavior:
- LfD captures the trajectory for one soil type on one slope
- VR captures decisions in one simulated scenario
- Expert systems capture what the operator can explain in an interview
- Video captures hours of undifferentiated operation

None of these transfer when conditions change. A new terrain, a different machine model, a transition from in-cab to remote operation, the move toward autonomous execution: all of these change the dynamics and invalidate condition-specific knowledge.

RAST would extract the underlying optimization strategy: this operator prioritizes fill efficiency and smoothness over speed, and they consistently manage stability margin more carefully than ground disturbance. Those priorities generate different specific behaviors on gravel versus clay versus rock, but the priorities themselves are stable.

## Data requirements

Minimum: the expert operating in 3 or more distinct conditions with 10 or more repetitions per condition. Total observation time estimated at 20-40 hours of active operation.

Sensor requirements: joint angles and velocities (from CAN bus), bucket/tool position (from GPS/IMU), hydraulic pressures (from CAN bus), and ideally load/force measurements. Modern equipment telemetry systems (Cat Product Link, Komatsu KOMTRAX, Volvo CareTrack) provide much of this data already.

## What RAST does NOT solve in this domain

- RAST does not replace the need for basic operator training
- RAST does not address visual perception and hazard recognition (that is the eye-tracking domain)
- RAST does not solve the sensor and communication challenges of remote operation (that is the 5G/haptics domain)
- RAST addresses the motor strategy layer specifically: what the expert optimizes for when controlling the machine
